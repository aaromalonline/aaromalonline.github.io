Ecometachip: A Low-Cost, Eco-Friendly Liquid Chemical Identifier

February 2026

This project introduces a biodegradable electronic system designed for identifying common liquids—water, ethanol, oils—based on variations in their relative permittivity ($\epsilon_r$). The core innovation involves re-engineering Coir-Rubber Composites (CoRC) from simple radio wave absorbers into active dielectric resonators.

The Substrate: Re-engineering CoRC

Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) reveals that the addition of carbon renders the CoRC matrix (coconut pith fiber and rubber latex) highly porous. While these pores traditionally increase power loss in shields, we leverage this porosity for fluid integration. Capillary action wicks target fluids into the matrix, displacing air and shifting the complex permittivity of the entire material.

Hardware Architecture: 100 MHz Hartley Oscillator

The sensor generates a continuous RF magnetic field. The self-resonant frequency of the LC tank is dictated by $f_o \propto 1/\sqrt{LC_{eff}}$. The CoRC patch functions as a dielectrically loaded secondary resonator. When permittivity shifts, it modulates the amplitude of the fluctuating magnetic field, which is captured non-contactivly by a secondary pickup coil.

Results & Linearity

Extensive hardware testing verified a sensitivity of **2.31 mV/$\epsilon_r$** with an $R^2$ score of **0.906**. The environmental robustness characterization revealed exceptional stability, with thermal drift constrained to 0.50 mV/°C. The system successfully enables analog classification via an RGB LED thresholding circuit.
